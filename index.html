SEC Semi-Annual Reporting Proposal Tracker (S7-2026-15)

In May 2026 the SEC issued a proposal that would allow public companies to switch from quarterly (Form 10-Q) to semi-annual financial reporting (a new Form 10-S). From the date of publication, the public has 60 days to comment on the proposal.

This site was produced by Professor Tzachi Zach at The Ohio State University Fisher College of Business as a public service to track the comment letters as they arrive, classify their positions, and surface patterns in the docket. A number of methodological decisions had to be made along the way — how to classify stance, how to bucket commenters by entity type, how to handle hedged or conditional letters — those decisions are explained below.

How stances are classified (three-rater LLM ensemble)
Each letter is independently classified under three different rubrics, and the headline stance shown on this site is the majority vote across the three. The literature on LLM-based annotation (Carlson, Rahmani & Chatterji, SMJ 2026; rubric-conditioned LLM labeling, CHB 2026) recommends multi-prompt validation rather than a single classifier call, and that is what we do here.
Rater 1 — Primary
Balanced read of the whole letter. Assigns Support / Oppose / Conditional based on the dominant position.
Rater 2 — Literalist
Defaults to Oppose unless the letter contains explicit, unconditional endorsement language ("I support…", "I urge the Commission to adopt…"). Conditional reserved for explicit "if X then yes" structure.
Rater 3 — Skeptic
Defaults to Conditional unless the letter is unambiguous. Any qualification, concession, or alternative proposal flips the call to Conditional.
Agreement across the three raters (N = 122):
  • Unanimous: 90 (73.8%)
  • 2-of-3 majority: 32 (26.2%)
  • Fleiss' κ: 0.476 · pairwise Cohen's κ: Primary–Literalist 0.55, Primary–Skeptic 0.60, Literalist–Skeptic 0.33

Each letter card in the table below carries an agreement badge (Unanimous / 2-of-3) and shows all three rater calls on hover.

Stance label conventions:
Oppose
Author argues against adoption.
Support
Author explicitly endorses the proposal.
Conditional
Author wants modifications or alternatives (e.g. enhanced auditor assurance, monthly revenue disclosure, every-4-months cadence, qualifying-criteria framework). Would not vote yes on the rule as written.
How commenters are classified by entity (three-rater LLM ensemble)
Letters are bucketed by who is writing, across eight categories. The taxonomy was refined with input from a colleague drawing on FASB comment-letter experience. As with stance, each letter is independently classified under three rubrics and the headline bucket is the majority of three.
The eight buckets:
  1. Individual — default dump bucket. We use "Individual" (not "Individual investor").
  2. Accountant (CPA) — CPA or chartered accountant credential, speaking from that professional lens.
  3. Issuer / Corporate — current — active corporate role (CFO, audit chair, financial reporting manager, etc.).
  4. Issuer / Corporate — former — retired or former executives writing personally. Plausibly different incentives from current insiders.
  5. Investment professional — active asset managers, hedge fund principals, RIAs, financial advisors.
  6. Academic researcher — university faculty appointment.
  7. Industry practitioner / technologist — non-academic professional roles outside corporate-issuer / investment-firm worlds (CISSP, software developer, IT auditor, compliance professional, etc.).
  8. Student — currently enrolled student.
Rater 1 — Primary
Balanced read. Uses the "follow the letterhead" principle as a tiebreaker — classify by the affiliation under which the writer is speaking, inferring institutional vs. personal voice from register, length, substance, and whether the institution is named for credibility or for attribution.
Rater 2 — Self-described
Takes the literal first identifier the writer offers. "CPA and retail investor" → Accountant; "Individual investor and former CFO" → Individual. No override based on context.
Rater 3 — Letterhead / functional
Overrides self-description with the strongest functional credential. Priority: current institutional role > former institutional role > formal professional credential > sector descriptor > self-id.
Agreement across the three raters (N = 122):
  • Unanimous: 111 (91.0%)
  • 2-of-3 majority: 11 (9.0%)
  • Fleiss' κ: 0.883 · pairwise Cohen's κ: Primary–Self-described 0.82, Primary–Letterhead 0.87, Self-described–Letterhead 0.95

Substantial agreement, notably higher than the stance ensemble (κ = 0.457). Majority headline distribution: Individual 81 / Accountant (CPA) 9 / Issuer-current 8 / Investment professional 8 / Industry practitioner 6 / Issuer-former 5 / Academic researcher 3 / Student 2.

How rationales are classified (argument taxonomy anchored on the SEC release)
Each comment letter can invoke one or more argument families. The taxonomy is anchored on the SEC's own framing in the proposing release (Release Nos. 33-11414; 34-105368; File No. S7-2026-15), with three commenter-distinctive codes added for arguments the SEC does not engage with as standalone justifications. 20 codes total: 16 SEC-engaged (9 anti-proposal, 5 pro-proposal, 1 conditional, 1 procedural), 3 commenter-distinctive (IP investor protection; US capital-market leadership; RI investor reliance interests), and 1 "no substantive rationale" for letters that state a position without engaging an argument.

Each anti-proposal code is rendered in a red shade and each pro-proposal code in a green shade so the directional balance is legible at a glance. Each SEC-engaged code is documented with a verbatim quote from the proposing release showing how the SEC itself frames that argument.
